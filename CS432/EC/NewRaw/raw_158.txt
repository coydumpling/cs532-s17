A separate spray may have been used as a diversion.</p><p>Mr Khalid has previously said the fact the woman who accosted Mr Kim immediately went to wash her hands showed she was "very aware" that she had been handling a toxin.</p><p>It would have begun affecting his nervous system immediately, causing first shaking and then death within minutes.</p><p><a href="/news/world-asia-39073837" class="story-body__link">More from expert Bruce Bennett</a></p><h2 class="story-body__crosshead">Is Kuala Lumpur airport safe?</h2><p>The authorities say they intend to decontaminate the airport and areas the suspects are known to have visited. </p><p>VX is a v-type nerve agent, which means the substance can remain lethal for a long period of time. </p><p>"It's as persistent as motor oil. It's going to stay there for a long time... which means anyone coming in contact with this could be intoxicated from it," forensic toxicologist John Trestrail told the Associated Press news agency.</p><p>No passengers, airport workers or medical staff who treated Mr Kim were reported to have become ill in the aftermath of the incident, the news agency adds.</p><p>Tens of thousands of passengers are believed to have passed through the airport since the attack more than 10 days ago. </p><h2 class="story-body__crosshead">Who was Kim Jong-nam?</h2><figure class="media-landscape has-caption full-width">
            <span class="image-and-copyright-container">
                
                
                <div class="js-delayed-image-load" data-alt="Kim Jong-nam" data-src="http://ichef.bbci.co.uk/news/320/cpsprodpb/A59E/production/_94789324_mediaitem94789323.jpg" data-width="976" data-height="549"></div>
                
                
                 <span class="off-screen">Image copyright</span>
                 <span class="story-image-copyright">AP</span>
                
            </span>
            
            <figcaption class="media-caption">
                <span class="off-screen">Image caption</span>
                <span class="media-caption__text">
                    North Korea has not identified the man who died as Kim Jong-nam, only as a North Korean citizen
                </span>
            </figcaption>
            
        </figure><p>The well-travelled and multilingual oldest son of late North Korean leader Kim Jong-il, he was once considered a potential future leader. He has lived abroad  for years and was bypassed in favour of his half-brother, Kim Jong-un.</p><p>He had been travelling on a passport under the name Kim Chol. North Korea has yet to confirm that the deceased was actually Kim Jong-nam.</p><p>For many years, it was believed Kim Jong-nam was being groomed to succeed his father as the next leader.</p><p>But that appears to have come to an end in 2001 when Kim was caught sneaking into Japan on a fake passport.</p><p>He later became one of the regime's most high-profile critics, openly questioning the Stalinist policies and dynastic succession his grandfather Kim Il-sung began crafting in 1948.</p><p><a href="/news/world-asia-38971834" class="story-body__link">Kim Jong-nam: North Korea's critic in exile</a></p><h2 class="story-body__crosshead">How did he die?</h2><p>A woman was seen in CCTV footage approaching Mr Kim and wiping something across his face. He sought medical help at the airport, saying someone had splashed or sprayed him with liquid. </p><p>He had a seizure and died on the way to hospital.</p><p>His body remains in the hospital's mortuary, amid a diplomatic dispute over who should claim it. </p><p><a href="/news/world-asia-39048796" class="story-body__link">Main players in mysterious killing</a></p><h2 class="story-body__crosshead">Who did it?</h2><p>Malaysia says it was clearly an attack by North Korean agents. Four people are in custody, including one North Korean and the two women he interacted with at the airport. Seven North Koreans are being sought, including a diplomat. </p><p>There are a number of North Korean organisations capable of directing such an attack, <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-asia-39073839" class="story-body__link">including the exclusive Guard Command</a>.</p><p>The North <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-asia-39062651" class="story-body__link">hit back at Malaysia on Thursday</a>, saying it was responsible for the death of one of its citizens.</p><p>In response, Malaysian Foreign Minister Anifah Aman warned North Korean envoy Kang Chol on Friday that he would be expelled unless he stopped "spewing lies" about the attack.</p><p><a href="/news/world-asia-39073839" class="story-body__link">Who in North Korea could organise a VX murder?</a></p><p><a href="/news/world-asia-38974455" class="story-body__link">North Korea's history of foreign assassinations</a></p><h2 class="story-body__crosshead">Does North Korea have VX?</h2><p>North Korea is one of just six countries not to have signed the <a href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-24082601" class="story-body__link">Chemical Weapons Convention (CWC)</a> arms control treaty banning the production, stockpiling and use of chemical weapons. </p><p>According to the <a href="http://www.nti.org/learn/countries/north-korea/chemical/" class="story-body__link-external">Nuclear Threat Initiative project</a>, however, North Korea is thought to have the third largest stockpile of chemical weapons, after the US and Russia.</p><p>South Korea's defence ministry estimated in 2014 that the North has somewhere between 2,500 and 5,000 tonnes of nerve agents in stock, with VX identified as among them.</p>
