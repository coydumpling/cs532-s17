<p class="first-para"><span class="first-sen">Trump administration spokesman <a href="https://www.whitehouse.gov/live/press-briefing-press-secretary-sean-spicer-10" target="_blank">Sean Spicer said</a>&nbsp;today that he believes "you'll see <a href="http://www.westword.com/news/trump-spokesman-predicts-greater-enforcement-of-federal-marijuana-laws-8823783" target="_blank">greater enforcement"</a> of federal drug laws in states that have legalized recreational marijuana.</span></p><div class="ads middle" data-vmg-ad="Middle" data-vmg-format="normal"></div><p>The remarks have harshed the mellow of an industry celebrating California's November passage of Proposition 64, which allows anyone 21 and older to legally possess up to one ounce of weed and will allow retail purchases of recreational weed starting in 2018. Marijuana entrepreneurs have been champing at the bit at the prospect of <a href="/news/will-marijuana-legalization-benefit-people-of-color-7535839" target="_blank">a projected $6.6 billion in legal pot revenue</a>&nbsp;in California by 2020.</p><p>If you'd like to envision what federal raids on otherwise legal marijuana enterprises in Los Angeles look like, you only have to go back a few years. In 2014 <a href="/news/dea-raids-four-la-weed-shops-4505245" target="_blank">the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration raided four medical dispensaries</a> in town. And in 2011 the DEA cracked down on medical pot shops in <a href="/news/marijuana-dea-raids-otherwise-legal-west-hollywood-pot-shops-wont-say-why-2384468" target="_blank">West Hollywood</a>, the <a href="/news/alleged-marijuana-growers-in-las-valley-targeted-by-dea-fbi-raids-2391278" target="_blank">San Fernando Valley and the Antelope Valley</a>. One Southern California dispensary operator was sentenced to <a href="/news/aaron-sanduskys-marijuana-dispensary-sales-get-him-10-year-federal-sentence-4175157" target="_blank">10 years in federal prison in 2013 for selling medical weed</a>. The&nbsp;Obama administration later softened enforcement, and in 2014 Obama signed into law <a href="/news/court-to-federal-anti-pot-crusaders-stfu-7272617" target="_blank">a cessation of funding for federal crackdowns in medical marijuana states</a>.</p><div id="ym_927393268424711308" class="ads centered yieldmo ym" data-vmg-ad="Yieldmo" data-yieldmo-id="927393268424711308"></div><p>"There were people who lost their freedom as a result of aggressive federal agents targeting key players," says&nbsp;Ethan Nadelmann, executive director of the Drug Policy Alliance. "This is a good time for everybody involved to batten down the hatches and move forward carefully and strategically."</p><div class="city-color-border related rel-stories"><h2 class="city-color">Related Stories</h2><ul><li><h3><a href="/news/marijuana-supporters-scared-shitless-by-trumps-pick-for-top-cop-7626416">Marijuana Supporters Scared Shitless by Trump's Pick for Top Cop</a></h3></li><li><h3><a href="/news/here-s-who-s-likely-to-reap-the-benefits-of-recreational-marijuana-in-california-6815468">Here&rsquo;s Who&rsquo;s Likely to Reap the Benefits of Recreational Marijuana in California</a></h3></li><li><h3><a href="/arts/5-dab-vapes-perfect-for-marijuana-on-the-go-7895494">5 Dab Vapes Perfect for Marijuana on the Go</a></h3></li></ul></div><p>Spicer differentiated between medical and recreational cannabis, saying that his boss "understands the pain and suffering that many people go through who are facing especially terminal diseases, and the comfort that some of these drugs, including medical marijuana, can bring to them." He also acknowledged that Congress has blocked Justice Department funding for crackdowns on state-recognized medical marijuana concerns.</p><p>Spicer likened marijuana legalization to the nation's opioid crisis, which many say is an inaccurate and unfair comparison. "When you see something like the opioid addiction crisis blossoming in so many states around this country, the last thing we should be doing is encouraging people," Spicer said.</p><span class="inline-video" data-vmg-ad="Teads" data-vmg-track="2nd ad"></span><p>His comments suggest that it soon could be open season on recreational cannabis businesses in states &mdash; including California, Colorado and Washington &ndash; that have fully legalized weed. "That's a very, very different subject," Spicer said of recreational pot.</p><p>How far the Trump administration will go is anyone's guess.&nbsp;Tom Angell, chairman of Marijuana Majority, noted in a statement today that "Trump clearly and repeatedly pledged that he would leave decisions on cannabis policy to the states." A&nbsp;Quinnipiac University national poll released today found that 71 percent of respondents believe the federal government should not enforce U.S. pot laws in states that have legalized medical or recreational cannabis. Fifty-nine percent said marijuana should be made fully legal in the United States.</p><p>According to New Frontier Data, which tracks pot revenues in states that have legalized it: "If the federal government decided to crack down on the adult-use side, this year alone, it could jeopardize $2.5 billion in projected revenue and $8.6 billion by 2020."</p>    <div class="side-tmaster">
