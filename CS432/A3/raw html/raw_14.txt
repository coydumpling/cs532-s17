</p><div itemprop="articleBody"><p class="mol-para-with-font"><span class="mol-style-bold">The Guinea Grill</span></p><p class="mol-para-with-font">30, Bruton Place, Mayfair,</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">London</p><p class="mol-ratings"><strong>Rating: <strong><img class="rating" src="http://i.dailymail.co.uk/i/articles/ratingStars/rating_showbiz_4.gif" width="55" /></strong></strong></p><p class="mol-para-with-font">The Guinea Grill peeks out from its immaculate Mayfair mews like a silk handkerchief tucked in the top pocket of an Anderson and Sheppard suit. It has little time for modish fads and churlish fashion, instead offering no-nonsense, straightforwardly stiff upper lip British tucker, seemingly unchanged since Nancy Sikes met her brutal end. At The Guinea Grill, the magnificently Edwardian meets the quietly modern. Because with its emphasis on well-brought-up beasts, it has its neat (but assuredly unmanicured) fingers placed soft upon the au courant pulse.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Masculine rather than metrosexual, and discreetly understated, this is a room where one succumbs to the manifold pleasures of the flesh  beef, mainly Scottish, hung in their own ageing room at the estimable Frank Godfrey butcher. Plus lamb, veal, venison, smoked salmon, Dover sole and pie.</p><div class="artSplitter mol-img-group">    <div class="mol-img">

</div>    <p class="imageCaption">At The Guinea Grill, the magnificently Edwardian meets the quietly modern</p></div><p class="mol-para-with-font">Like all restaurants entirely confident in their own skin, they feel no need to bang on about breed and provenance. If you eat here, they say, put your faith in our good taste. The service is smart, slick and old-school  authoritative but never condescending.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">At the helm, Oisin Rogers, genial and velvet-clad, a modern master of the front of house. He brings a breath of fresh air to the Guinea, but is wise enough to know exactly why it works. Its a restaurant that offers consistency and charm, a place where pies always please, and steaks never let you down.</p><div><div class="related-carousel with-fb event half"

</div><p class="mol-para-with-font">There are two cosily crammed rooms, with tables, draped in immaculate white linen, stuffed politely together like bowler-hatted commuters on the Clapham omnibus. The walls are partly wood-panelled, the carpets tartan and the punters overwhelmingly male. Stuffed game sit in glass cases, while oil paintings are filled with shaggy Highland cattle, ducks and wildly Romantic vistas. But red is the colour that permeates all, red in tooth and claw red, on the walls and in the beef, the burgundy and the claret.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Im here with Andrew Duckworth, a man who generously bid in a charity auction to take lunch with me. Dear God, he must have been merry to want to dine with this scruffy hack (Im the only person not wearing a suit), but his bank balances loss is my gain. Hes a fine lunch companion, a legend of the North West, and a serious eater, too. We discuss mutual friends, mainly the Ribble Valley mafia, and order prawn cocktail and smoked haddock with egg. The former coats cool, curvy, fecund North Sea prawns in a piquant slick of pink sauce, and plonks them atop a tangle of iceberg lettuce. No frills or flourishes, no irony or retro winks, just a great dish, unmucked-about. My haddock wears its smoke lightly and lovingly, falling apart into succulent chunks, coated with oozing egg and crisp slivers of fried chorizo.</p><div class="artSplitter mol-img-group">    <div class="mol-img" style="height: auto;">

</div>    <p class="imageCaption">Like all restaurants entirely confident in their own skin, they feel no need to bang on about breed and provenance. If you eat here, they say, put your faith in our good taste</p></div><p class="mol-para-with-font">Rib-eye steak is lustily run through with fat, its surface charred, its interior winsomely pink. Good steak, as youd expect. Made better by two fried eggs, perched proudly on top. I just had to have them, sighs Andrew. A man very much after my own heart. The sides frolic happily in more fat  spinach draped in melted gruyre; minted peas glossy with butter; browned and bubbling gratin potatoes. Oh, and the best (and most lavishly buttered) carrots Ive ever eaten, a freebie from Oisin, made to his mothers recipe.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font">My Guinea Grill (note the epic capitalisation), though, is no mere meaty mlange, rather a belly-busting beauty, a carnivorous cavalcade of heady Barnum-esque brilliance. Theres a lamb chop, the flesh torn from the bone in one joyous bite. And sausage, plump, herby and as steadfastly English as any Anthony Trollope Warden. A fat morsel of rare, minerally sirloin steak nestles next to a great peppery slab of ox heart, reassuringly chewy and surprisingly subtle. Beef liver is a little more, well, robust than the usual lamb but what it lacks in delicacy, it makes up for in flavour. Then a single lambs kidney, expertly trimmed, exquisitely fresh and lusciously pert. A couple of slices of bacon rib steak add smoke and salt, while a fried egg, garlicky field mushroom and properly grilled tomato are almost lost in this marvellous morass. Phew and hallelujah. This is a dish of London legend, the Ozymandias of mixed grills. Look on my works, ye mighty, and despair. I just about make it to the end, exalted.</p><div class="artSplitter mol-img-group">    <div class="mol-img">

</div>    <p class="imageCaption">Rib-eye steak</p></div><p class="mol-para-with-font">Pudding is an impossibility. Obviously. We just about manage to force down a couple of strictly medicinal Calvados and linger, as grey afternoon turns inevitably to gloomy gloaming. You know, I say, belly spilling over unhitched belt, we never did get to try that steak and kidney pie. Andrew nods. Theres always next time, he says. I smile. And bow my head in silent prayer. Thank the Lord for the Guinea Grill.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><span class="mol-style-italic">Lunch for two: 110</span></p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><span class="mol-style-bold mol-style-medium"><span class="news-ccox">What Tom ate this week</span></span></p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><span class="mol-style-bold debate-ccox">Tuesday</span></p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Off to the V&amp;A to celebrate my cousin Ben joining the board. Then to San Lorenzo, where the food is markedly better than it once was. Crisp fried squid, then Pasta Schillaci, named after that spicy hero of Italia 90.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><span class="mol-style-bold"><span class="debate-ccox">Wednesday</span></span></p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Lunch with the great Fay Maschler at The Wolseley. Chopped liver, smoked salmon, steak tartare and kedgeree. Perfect, in every way. Dinner with my deputy editor at The Princess Victoria. Decent steak. The food has improved again.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><span class="debate-ccox mol-style-bold">Thursday</span></p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Dinner of a peppery Sri Lankan curry, from a recipe by the Curry Guy. Chicken thighs and lots of spice. Wonderful.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font"><span class="mol-style-bold debate-ccox">Friday</span></p><p class="mol-para-with-font">Lunch with Simon Hopkinson at Six Portland Road. Halibut with monks beard and bottarga. It really is the perfect neighbourhood restaurant. And for dinner? A toastedcheese sandwich.</p><p class="mol-para-with-font"></p></div>
