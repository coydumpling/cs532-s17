<div class="item-obsession item-meta-row">		
	<h5>Obsession</h5>
	<a href="https://qz.com/on/life-as-laboratory/">Life as Laboratory</a>
</div>

<div class="item-timestamp item-meta-row">

			<span class="timestamp" itemprop="datePublished">February 19, 2017</span>
	
	
</div>
	</div>
	<div class="item-body" itemprop="articleBody">
		<div>
			
<p class="p1"><span class="s1">Look at a photo of yourself as a teenager and, mistaken fashion choices aside, it&rsquo;s likely you see traces of the same person with the same personality quirks as you are today. But whether or not you truly are the same person over a lifetime&mdash;and what that notion of personhood even means&mdash;is the subject of ongoing philosophical and psychology debate.</span></p>
<p class="p1"><span class="s1">The longest personality study of all time, <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5144810/"><span class="s2">published in Psychology and Aging</span></a> and <a href="https://digest.bps.org.uk/2017/02/07/longest-ever-personality-study-finds-no-correlation-between-measures-taken-at-age-14-and-age-77/"><span class="s2">recently highlighted</span></a> by the British Psychological Society, suggests that over the course of a lifetime, just as your physical appearance changes and <a href="http://science.howstuffworks.com/life/cellular-microscopic/does-body-really-replace-seven-years.htm">your cells are constantly replaced</a>, your personality is also transformed beyond recognition.</span></p>
<p class="p1"><span class="s1">The study begins with data from a 1950 survey of 1,208 14-year-olds in Scotland. Teachers were asked to use six questionnaires to rate the teenagers on six personality traits: self-confidence, perseverance, stability of moods, conscientiousness, originality, and desire to learn. Together, the results from these questionnaires were amalgamated into a rating for one trait, which was defined as &ldquo;dependability.&rdquo; More than six decades later, researchers tracked down 635 of the participants, and 174 agreed to repeat testing.</span></p>
<div class="inline-ad" id="inline-914002-1"></div><p class="p1"><span class="s1">This time, aged 77 years old, the participants rated themselves on the six personality traits, and also nominated a close friend or relative to do the same. Overall, there was not much overlap from the questionnaires taken 63 years earlier.<span class="Apple-converted-space">  </span>&ldquo;Correlations suggested no significant stability of any of the 6 characteristics or their underlying factor, dependability, over the 63-year interval,&rdquo; wrote the researchers. &ldquo;We hypothesized that we would find evidence of personality stability over an even longer period of 63 years, but our correlations did not support this hypothesis,&rdquo; they later added.</span></p>
<p class="p1"><span class="s1">The findings were a surprise to researchers because previous personality studies, over shorter periods of time, seemed to show consistency. Studies over several decades, focusing on participants from <a href="http://www.apple.com"><span class="s2">childhood to middle age</span></a>, or from <a href="http://psycnet.apa.org/psycinfo/1986-11794-001"><span class="s2">middle age to older age,</span></a> showed stable personality traits. But the most recent study, covering the longest period, suggests that personality stability is disrupted over time. &ldquo;The longer the interval between two assessments of personality, the weaker the relationship between the two tends to be,&rdquo; the researchers write. &ldquo;Our results suggest that, when the interval is increased to as much as 63 years, there is hardly any relationship at all.&rdquo;</span></p>
<p class="p1"><span class="s1">Perhaps those who had impulsive character flaws as a teenager would be grateful that certain personality traits might even out later in life. But it&rsquo;s disconcerting to think that your entire personality is transformed. </span></p>
<p class="p1"><span class="s1">&ldquo;Personality refers to an individual&rsquo;s characteristic patterns of thought, emotion, and behavior, together with the psychological mechanisms&mdash;hidden or not&mdash;behind those patterns,&rdquo; note the authors, quoting psychology professor David Funder&rsquo;s <a href="https://www.amazon.com/Personality-Puzzle-Sixth-David-Funder/dp/039312441X?tag=quartz07-20"><span class="s2">definition</span></a>. </span></p>
<div class="inline-ad" id="inline-914002-2"></div><p class="p1"><span class="s1">If your patterns of thought, emotions, and behavior so drastically alter over the decades, can you truly be considered the same person in old age as you were as a teenager? This question ties in with broader theories about the nature of the self. For example, t<a href="https://qz.com/506229/neuroscience-backs-up-the-buddhist-belief-that-the-self-isnt-constant-but-ever-changing/"><span class="s2">here is growing neuroscience research</span></a> that supports the ancient Buddhist belief that our notion of a stable &ldquo;self&rdquo; is nothing more than an illusion.</span></p>
<p class="p1"><span class="s1">Perhaps this won&rsquo;t surprise you if you&rsquo;ve had the experience of running into a very old friend from school, and found a completely different person from the child you remembered. This research suggests that, as the decades go by, your own younger self could be similarly unrecognizable. </span></p>
